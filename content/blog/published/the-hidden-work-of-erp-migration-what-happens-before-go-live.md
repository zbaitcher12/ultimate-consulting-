Most conversations about ERP migration focus on go-live.

The go-live date. The go-live readiness review. The go-live communications plan. The go-live support structure.

What receives far less attention—and what actually determines whether go-live succeeds or fails—is the work that happens before it. The preparation phase of an ERP migration is where the real complexity lives. And in higher education, that complexity is almost always underestimated.

---

## Data Cleanup Is the Work Nobody Wants to Do

Every higher education institution that has migrated an ERP system has discovered the same thing: their data is not as clean as they thought.

Decades of manual data entry, inconsistent field use, workarounds built on top of workarounds, and undocumented customizations accumulate in legacy systems. When that data is mapped to a new ERP, the inconsistencies surface in ways that are both technically complex and operationally urgent.

Common data problems that emerge in pre-implementation include:

* Duplicate student records created across departments over years of parallel data entry
* Financial aid disbursement history that doesn’t reconcile with general ledger records
* Course catalog data that reflects retired programs never formally removed from the system
* HR records with inconsistent field values that block automated workflow rules in the new platform
* Address and identity data that doesn’t meet the validation standards of the new system

Data cleanup is unglamorous work. It requires dedicated staff capacity, functional expertise from every affected department, and institutional commitment to completing it—even when competing priorities make it tempting to defer.

Institutions that defer data cleanup to post-go-live are making a decision: they are choosing to go live with problems rather than resolving them before the system is live. That decision compounds. Every problematic record that enters the new system during go-live is a problem that must be resolved later at higher cost.

## Process Redesign Must Happen Before Configuration, Not After

One of the most consequential mistakes in ERP pre-implementation is configuring the new system to replicate the workflows of the old one.

This happens because it feels safe. Familiar workflows create less resistance. Staff who understand how the current system works can configure the new one to behave similarly, reducing the learning curve and the change management burden.

But replicating the past on a new platform wastes the transformation opportunity that ERP migration represents. The new system has different native workflow logic, different integration capabilities, and different reporting structures. Forcing it to behave like the old system means accepting the limitations of the old system on a platform with better native capabilities.

Process redesign before configuration means:

* Documenting current workflows not to replicate them but to understand what institutional need each workflow serves
* Evaluating which workflows exist because of institutional policy and which exist because of legacy system constraints
* Designing new workflows around the native capabilities of the new platform
* Securing agreement from functional leaders before configuration begins—not after

This work is difficult. It requires functional experts to challenge assumptions about how work has always been done. It requires leadership to accept temporary disruption in exchange for long-term efficiency. But it is the work that determines whether the new system enables transformation or merely preserves the status quo on newer hardware.

## Cross-Department Collaboration Is a Prerequisite, Not a Nice-to-Have

ERP systems in higher education span the entire institution. Financial aid. Registration. Student accounts. Human resources. Finance. Academic affairs. Each of these functions has its own data, its own workflows, its own compliance requirements, and its own definition of what a successful implementation looks like.

When these functions plan their ERP implementation independently—which is the default in most institutions—the integrations between them reveal gaps that no individual function anticipated.

Financial aid data structures that don’t align with general ledger configuration. Registration term models that conflict with academic planning assumptions. Student services workflows that depend on data fields that IT has configured differently.

Cross-department collaboration during pre-implementation is not about consensus building. It is about identifying integration dependencies before they become go-live failures. It requires regular joint sessions where functional teams review configuration decisions in context, not in isolation.

---

## Governance Planning Before Go-Live Determines Post-Go-Live Stability

The governance structures that support an ERP implementation don’t disappear at go-live. They evolve. But institutions that haven’t planned for post-go-live governance during the pre-implementation phase are consistently caught unprepared when stabilization issues arise.

Pre-go-live governance planning includes defining who has the authority to approve configuration changes after go-live, how support requests are prioritized and resolved during the stabilization period, and what the escalation path is when functional areas conflict over system behavior.

It also includes planning for how the implementation team transitions to a sustainable support model—because the intensity of implementation staffing cannot be maintained indefinitely, and the transition to normal operations is itself a risk point.

---

## A Leadership Question Worth Asking Now

The work that happens before go-live is invisible in project dashboards and implementation status reports. It doesn’t generate the milestone celebration that go-live does.

But it is the work that determines whether go-live is a triumph or a crisis—and whether the new system delivers the institutional value the migration was designed to produce.

**If your ERP went live tomorrow, how confident are you that your institution’s data and processes are truly ready?**

That confidence is earned in the pre-implementation phase. And the institutions that invest in it early consistently perform better at every stage that follows.
