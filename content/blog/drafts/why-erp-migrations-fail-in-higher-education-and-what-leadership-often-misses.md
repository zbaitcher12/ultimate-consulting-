Higher education institutions invest millions in ERP migration projects.

They invest far less in understanding why those projects so frequently fall short.

The reasons ERP migrations fail in higher education are well-documented in post-mortems and consulting after-action reports. They are not mysteries. They are patterns—predictable, recurring, and almost always traceable back to decisions made at the leadership level, not the implementation level.

Understanding those patterns is the first step toward breaking them.

---

## Leadership Misalignment Is the Root Cause Most Institutions Don’t Name

Ask most higher education leaders why an ERP migration struggled, and you’ll hear answers about vendors, timelines, and data complexity.

Those are symptoms. The root cause is almost always leadership misalignment.

ERP migration in higher education requires sustained, coordinated commitment from the president, CFO, CIO, enrollment VP, financial aid director, registrar, and provost—simultaneously. When any of these leaders treats the migration as primarily someone else’s responsibility, the gaps that result create project risk that no implementation team can fully absorb.

Common manifestations of leadership misalignment include:

* Cabinet members who delegate ERP decisions to functional staff without providing the authority those staff need to make binding decisions across departments
* CIOs who own the technology project but don’t own the change management required for the institution to actually use the new system
* Financial aid directors and registrars brought into planning conversations after key architecture decisions are already locked in
* Presidents who signal ERP migration as a priority but don’t protect the staff capacity, budget, and decision-making timelines required to move it forward

Alignment doesn’t mean everyone agrees on every decision. It means everyone understands the institutional goals driving the migration, the timeline implications for their area, and the decisions they are accountable for making.

---

## Unrealistic Expectations Create Risk That Compounds Over Time

The second most common failure driver is unrealistic expectations—about timelines, about the complexity of data migration, about the gap between a system’s out-of-the-box functionality and the institution’s actual workflows, and about how much staff capacity an ERP migration truly requires.

Higher education ERP migrations are among the most complex technology transitions an institution undertakes. They touch every major operational function—financial aid, registration, human resources, finance, student services, academic affairs. They require data migration from systems that may have accumulated decades of customization, inconsistent data entry practices, and undocumented processes.

When leadership expects an ERP migration to deliver a new system with full functionality on the original timeline and within the original budget, they are often setting the institution up for a difficult conversation midway through the project.

Realistic expectations mean acknowledging that:

* Data cleanup typically takes longer than projected
* Custom functionality built on legacy systems may not have direct equivalents in the new platform
* Staff capacity requirements during go-live periods are significantly higher than during normal operations
* Post-go-live stabilization, not go-live itself, is when the real work of making the system functional begins

---

## Poor Governance Structures Make Problems Permanent

ERP migration projects that lack clear governance structures don’t just encounter problems—they encounter problems that no one has the authority to resolve.

Effective ERP governance in higher education requires:

* A steering committee with cabinet-level representation and genuine decision-making authority
* Clear protocols for escalation when cross-departmental conflicts arise
* A project owner with institutional accountability—not just a vendor project manager
* Defined criteria for scope decisions, so that scope creep is evaluated against institutional priorities rather than departmental preferences

Without this structure, ERP migrations accumulate unresolved decisions. Unresolved decisions become project delays. Project delays become budget overruns. And budget overruns become the crisis that finally forces a governance conversation that should have happened before the contract was signed.

---

## Change Management Is Not a Line Item—It’s the Work

The most expensive mistake higher education institutions make in ERP migration is treating change management as a training deliverable rather than an institutional investment.

Change management in an ERP context is not a series of training sessions delivered before go-live. It is the sustained effort to help every functional area of the institution understand why the system is changing, how their workflows will change, and what support they will have to navigate the transition.

It includes stakeholder communication that starts at project kickoff and continues through post-go-live stabilization. It includes functional champions in every major affected department. It includes leadership who model adoption by using the new system—not by requesting reports from the old one while staff struggle with the new interface.

When change management is underfunded, the institution gets a technically implemented system that nobody uses effectively. That is not a technology success. It is a transformation failure.

---

## A Leadership Question Worth Asking Now

ERP migrations that succeed do so because institutional leadership treats them as institutional transformation initiatives—not technology projects with a go-live date.

The technology matters. The vendor matters. The implementation methodology matters.

But none of those things matter more than the quality of the leadership decisions made before the contract is signed, the governance structures in place when the first conflict arises, the realistic expectations set in the boardroom about what this migration will require, and the change management investment that determines whether the new system actually changes how the institution operates.

**Is your ERP migration being treated as a technology project—or an institutional transformation initiative?**

That distinction determines everything that follows.
