The technology in an ERP migration is the most visible part of the project.

It is not the most important part.

The technology—the platform, the configuration, the data migration, the integrations—creates the conditions for institutional improvement. But whether those conditions actually produce improvement depends entirely on whether the people using the system change how they work.

Change management is the function that makes that happen. And in higher education ERP migrations, it is consistently the most underfunded, underplanned, and undervalued component of the project.

---

## Why Technology Implementation Is Not the Same as Organizational Change

Technology implementation ends when the system is live. Organizational change begins there.

This distinction matters because the investment models for these two activities are different. Technology implementation has a defined scope, a defined timeline, and a deliverable that can be tested and validated. Organizational change is diffuse, ongoing, and dependent on hundreds of individual decisions that no project plan can fully anticipate.

Institutions that conflate these two activities budget for one and get surprised by the other. They invest adequately in the technology implementation and discover, post-go-live, that the system is live but the institution is not actually operating differently. Staff have learned to navigate the new interface while preserving the workflows of the old system. Leadership is receiving the same reports, extracted through the same manual processes, from a more expensive platform.

This is the change management gap. And it is not a technology problem. It is a leadership and investment problem.

## What Organizational Culture Actually Requires in ERP Transition

Higher education institutional culture is strong. Established ways of working are deeply embedded in professional identity, departmental relationships, and informal institutional knowledge that has accumulated over years.

This is not a pathology. Institutional culture is what creates continuity, reliability, and professional expertise. But in an ERP migration context, it also creates resistance—not malicious resistance, but the natural human response to being asked to work differently when the current way of working is familiar, comfortable, and, from the staff member’s perspective, effective.

Effective change management in ERP migration addresses this resistance directly:

* **By communicating the why before the how.** Staff who understand why the institution is migrating—what institutional goals the migration serves, what problems it is designed to solve—are better positioned to accept the disruption it requires than staff who are simply informed that the system is changing.
* **By involving staff in configuration decisions.** When functional staff have input into how the new system is configured for their workflows, they have ownership of those decisions that significantly reduces resistance at go-live.
* **By creating functional champions.** Staff in each major affected area who receive deeper training, are positioned as peer resources, and are empowered to provide in-the-moment support during the stabilization period dramatically accelerate adoption.
* **By addressing resistance as information.** Staff who resist the new system are often carrying legitimate concerns about workflow gaps, training deficiencies, or configuration problems that the implementation team needs to know about. Treating resistance as feedback rather than obstruction produces better implementation outcomes.

## Leadership Modeling Is the Most Powerful Change Management Signal

In higher education, as in most organizations, staff observe what leaders do as closely as they listen to what leaders say.

Leadership behavior during and after ERP migration is the most powerful change management signal the institution sends. When cabinet members and department heads use the new system, request reports through the new platform, and visibly engage with the new workflows—staff receive an unambiguous signal that the new system is the operating standard.

When leaders continue to request manual reports, route exceptions outside the system, or express skepticism about the new platform’s capabilities in meetings where staff are present—the change management message is equally clear: the new system is optional for those with enough institutional standing to avoid it.

This is not a technology problem. It is a leadership accountability problem. And it is solvable—by leaders who understand that their behavior during ERP transition is itself a change management investment.

---

## A Leadership Question Worth Asking Now

ERP success is determined more by organizational change than by technology configuration. The platform matters. The implementation matters. But neither of them matters as much as whether the people using the system—and the leaders modeling its use—genuinely change how they work.

**Are your staff prepared to work differently—or simply expected to adapt to new systems?**

There is a significant difference between those two things. Preparation is an institutional investment. Expectation without preparation is an institutional risk. And the institutions that confuse them consistently underperform on ERP implementations that were technically sound but organizationally unprepared.
