Go-live is the milestone every ERP migration team works toward.

It is celebrated with announcement emails, congratulatory messages from leadership, and often a genuine sense of relief that the most intense phase of the project is over.

It is also one of the most dangerous moments in an ERP implementation.

Not because go-live is bad. But because the belief that go-live is the end—that the work is done when the system is live—is one of the most reliably damaging misconceptions in higher education technology.

---

## What Actually Happens at Go-Live

Go-live in an ERP migration means that the institution has successfully transitioned from the old system to the new one—that data has been migrated, workflows have been configured, staff have been trained, and the system is processing real institutional transactions.

What go-live does not mean is that the system is optimized, that staff are fully proficient, that integrations are stable, that exception handling processes are established, or that the institutional capability the migration was designed to create has been realized.

What actually happens at go-live is that a new set of challenges begins. Staff encounter real-world transactions that training simulations didn’t cover. Integrations that performed correctly in testing reveal edge cases that only emerge under production volume. Data migration decisions that seemed correct in testing produce unexpected results in actual operations. Configuration assumptions that worked in isolation reveal conflicts when multiple functions use the system simultaneously.

These are not implementation failures. They are the predictable features of putting a complex system into full operational use. But they require a response capacity that many institutions have already disbanded by the time they emerge.

---

## The Stabilization Period: Why It Requires Dedicated Investment

The period immediately following go-live—typically the first three to six months of full operation—is the stabilization period. This is the phase when the gap between configured system and optimized system is most acute, when staff proficiency is lowest relative to system capability, and when the institutional cost of operational disruption is highest.

Institutions that invest in the stabilization period do so through:

* Maintaining elevated implementation staffing through the first full operational cycle after go-live
* Establishing a formal issue tracking and resolution process with clear prioritization criteria
* Providing sustained post-go-live training that addresses real operational scenarios, not simulated ones
* Conducting regular cross-functional stabilization reviews that identify systemic issues before they become entrenched workarounds
* Retaining vendor and partner support at a level appropriate for stabilization, not just implementation

Institutions that scale back implementation resources immediately at go-live—or that transition abruptly to standard IT support models before stabilization is complete—consistently experience longer stabilization periods, more entrenched workarounds, and lower long-term system adoption.

## Adoption: The Measure of ERP Success That Go-Live Doesn’t Capture

The true measure of ERP success is not whether the system is live. It is whether the institution is using the system to operate more effectively than it did before.

Adoption is the measure that captures this. And adoption in an ERP context means more than staff logging in to the new system. It means:

* Staff using the system’s native workflows rather than building shadow processes in spreadsheets and email
* Leadership using the system’s reporting rather than requesting manual data extracts
* Exception handling routed through the system rather than outside it
* New hires being onboarded to the new system rather than to informal workarounds that experienced staff have developed

Adoption is earned through post-go-live investment in training, process reinforcement, and leadership modeling. It is not automatic. And the institutions that measure it explicitly—and invest in it deliberately—extract significantly more value from their ERP migration than those that declare success at go-live and move on.

---

## Optimization: The Phase Where ERP Investment Pays Off

Beyond stabilization and adoption lies optimization—the phase when institutions move from using the system competently to using it strategically.

Optimization means identifying the workflows that can be automated, the reports that can be operationalized, the integrations that can be built, and the capabilities that can be unlocked by staff who now understand the system well enough to use it beyond its initial configuration.

This is the phase where ERP investment pays off. And it only becomes accessible to institutions that have invested in stabilization and adoption first.

---

## A Leadership Question Worth Asking Now

The go-live myth is costly not because go-live doesn’t matter, but because treating it as the end redirects the resources, attention, and investment that the post-go-live phase requires.

ERP migration success is measured years after go-live—in whether the system is fully adopted, whether workarounds have been eliminated, whether the institutional capabilities the migration was designed to create have actually been realized.

**Is your ERP go-live the finish line—or the beginning of transformation?**

The institutions that answer that question correctly invest accordingly. And the investment pays off in ERP value that institutions treating go-live as an endpoint never capture.
