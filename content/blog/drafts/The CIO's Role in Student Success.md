When campus leaders talk about student success, they usually picture advisors, faculty, financial aid counselors, and student affairs professionals.

Rarely does the conversation start with IT.

It should.

Your retention strategy is only as strong as the systems underneath it.

The early-alert tool, the advising platform, the LMS, the CRM, the SIS, the financial aid portal, every one of them shapes whether a student makes it from week one to graduation.

That makes the CIO one of the most influential leaders in your student success ecosystem, whether or not the org chart says so.

## The Old Story: IT as the Plumbing

For years, IT's role in student success was framed as plumbing: keep the systems running, fix things when they break, handle integrations behind the scenes.

Useful, but not strategic.

That story made sense when technology touched the student journey only at the edges, mainly at registration and grades.

It does not make sense today, when the student experience is delivered through technology end to end:

- Pre-enrollment: targeted communications, application portals, financial aid simulations
- Onboarding: orientation, course planning, advising scheduling
- Term: LMS, attendance tracking, early-alert signals
- Support: counseling, tutoring, bursar payments
- Persistence: re-enrollment campaigns, degree progress, transfer credit
- Completion: graduation audits, transcripts, alumni handoff

Every touchpoint is technology-enabled.

Every gap in those systems is a retention risk.

## The New Mandate: CIO as Student Success Partner

When CIOs step fully into a student success role, three things change:

### 1. Technology decisions are made through a student lens

Instead of asking "what's the best HR or finance system," the question becomes "what does this decision do to the student experience?"

That single shift sharpens every roadmap and every vendor choice.

### 2. Cross-functional data finally connects

Retention is not a single number, it's a hundred small signals:

- Missed classes
- Unpaid balances
- Declining LMS engagement
- Advising no-shows
- Unanswered emails

Only IT can stitch those signals together across systems so academic and student affairs leaders can act.

### 3. Service design becomes strategy

Why does it take six clicks to get to a financial aid status?

Why are emails from advising and the bursar contradicting each other?

Why does the mobile app crash during peak registration?

Each of these is both an IT problem and a retention problem, and the CIO who treats them as both becomes indispensable.

## What Strategic CIO Leadership Looks Like

The most effective student-success-aligned CIOs share a few practices:

- They sit on the student success steering team, not just on tech committees.
- They translate technology choices into student impact language for the cabinet and the board.
- They invest in integration and identity as core student experience capabilities.
- They measure IT success partly through retention, persistence, and student satisfaction metrics, not just uptime.
- They build relationships with deans, the dean of students, advising leaders, and the registrar that are as deep as their relationships with vendors.

## Action Steps for Higher Ed Leaders

For presidents, provosts, and CIOs ready to make this shift, three concrete steps tend to unlock progress:

### Invite IT into the student success conversation early

Don't bring CIOs in at the end to "make it work."

Bring them in at the beginning to shape what should be built.

### Pick two or three student experience moments and redesign them end-to-end

Aid award delivery, advising scheduling, registration, each is a chance to demonstrate IT-led student success in action.

### Tie a few IT scorecard metrics to student outcomes

Persistence rates, mobile experience scores, time-to-first-advisor-meeting.

Make student success visible in IT's performance story.

## Why This Matters Now

Enrollment headwinds, public scrutiny, and tightening budgets are forcing every institution to do more with less.

The institutions that will pull ahead are the ones whose CIOs are sitting at the table when student success is being designed, not waiting in another room for tickets.

## Final Thought

How often is IT in the room when your campus is talking about retention and student success?

If the answer is "rarely," the most valuable upgrade on your roadmap might not be a system.

It might be a seat at the table.
