Ask anyone who has led an ERP migration in higher education about the hardest part, and the answer is almost always the same.

Data.

Not the vendor selection. Not the implementation methodology. Not the go-live coordination. The data—its quality, its governance, and the complexity of moving it from legacy systems into a new platform that has different structural assumptions about how institutional information should be organized.

Data is the hardest part of ERP migration because it is the place where the gap between how an institution believes it operates and how it actually operates becomes impossible to ignore.

---

## Data Governance: The Foundation Most Institutions Haven’t Built

Data governance is the set of decisions an institution makes about who owns its data, what those terms mean, and who is accountable for data quality.

Most higher education institutions have not built this foundation. Data that lives in ERP systems has been created, modified, and interpreted differently across departments over decades. The financial aid office has a definition of “enrollment” for FAFSA purposes. The registrar has a definition for academic record purposes. The enrollment VP has a definition for yield tracking purposes. And the three definitions produce different numbers when extracted from the same system.

This is a governance problem before it is a data problem. When data governance is absent, no amount of data migration technical expertise can compensate. The institution will move its inconsistencies from the old system to the new one—and the new system will surface them in ways the old one may have obscured.

ERP migration is the forcing function that makes data governance a priority. The institutions that treat it that way—that use migration as the opportunity to finally define their data standards, assign data ownership, and resolve definitional inconsistencies across departments—emerge from implementation with a data foundation that serves them for years. The institutions that defer governance work to post-migration cleanup inherit a new system on top of an unresolved data problem.

---

## Data Quality: The Problems That Emerge Under Migration Pressure

Even institutions with reasonable governance frameworks discover data quality problems during ERP migration that they did not know they had.

Legacy ERP systems in higher education are typically configured to tolerate data entry inconsistencies that modern platforms reject. Records that have blank required fields. Values that don’t conform to reference table standards. Dates that are formatted differently across entry periods. Student identifiers that have been duplicated or modified in ways that violate uniqueness constraints.

When this data is extracted for migration, the volume of problematic records is almost always larger than the pre-migration data audit estimated. Common sources of data quality problems include:

* Department-level data entry practices that varied over time and across staff turnover
* System customizations that created non-standard field usage
* Conversion artifacts from previous migrations that were never cleaned up
* Records that were closed but not archived, remaining active in the system without current maintenance

Addressing data quality problems during migration requires more than technical remediation. It requires functional expertise to make authoritative decisions about what the correct values should be. Those decisions require staff capacity and institutional authority that must be planned for explicitly—not improvised during a go-live timeline crunch.

---

## Data Migration Complexity: Why Technical Competence Is Necessary But Not Sufficient

Data migration in an ERP context is a technical challenge. The tools, methods, and expertise required to extract data from legacy systems, transform it into the target system’s data model, and load it with appropriate validation are specialized and consequential.

But technical competence in data migration is necessary, not sufficient.

The most challenging data migration problems in higher education ERP transitions are not technical. They are definitional. They arise when the source system organized information one way and the target system organizes it differently—and someone has to decide how the transformation should work.

Those decisions require domain expertise from financial aid, registration, student accounts, and finance, not just from IT. They require institutional authority to make binding decisions about data mapping. And they require documentation that preserves the rationale for those decisions, because questions about migrated data will arise long after the individuals who made the decisions have moved on.

---

## A Leadership Question Worth Asking Now

Data quality, data governance, and data migration complexity are not problems that can be solved by a vendor or an implementation partner alone. They require institutional investment, institutional authority, and institutional accountability.

The institutions that invest in this work before migration—that build their data governance frameworks, complete their data quality audits, and staff their migration teams with functional domain expertise—consistently produce better migration outcomes than those that treat data as a technical deliverable.

**If your institution migrated systems tomorrow, how confident are you in the quality of the data being moved?**

That confidence is not a technical measurement. It is an institutional one. And it is earned through the governance and quality work that happens long before the migration clock starts running.
