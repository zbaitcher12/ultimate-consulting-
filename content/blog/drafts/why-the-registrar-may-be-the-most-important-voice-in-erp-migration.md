ERP migration conversations in higher education tend to gravitate toward the CIO, the CFO, and increasingly the enrollment VP.

The registrar is often an afterthought.

This is a costly mistake. The registrar’s office is the custodian of the data that defines academic reality for every student at the institution. Curriculum structures. Term models. Transfer credit frameworks. Degree audit logic. Academic record architecture. Graduation requirements. The configuration decisions that govern these structures in an ERP system are among the most consequential in the entire implementation—and they are decisions that only a registrar can make with the necessary institutional knowledge and academic policy authority.

---

## Curriculum and Term Model Architecture Shapes Everything Downstream

The way an institution organizes its academic programs, course structures, and term models in an ERP system determines how that data behaves everywhere else in the platform.

Enrollment management reporting depends on how the SIS defines a student’s enrollment status—which depends on how courses, credits, and terms are configured. Financial aid packaging logic depends on credit-hour definitions and enrollment intensity calculations that flow from curriculum configuration. Retention analytics depend on consistent academic progress measurement that requires well-designed term model architecture.

When curriculum and term model configuration is done without the registrar’s leadership, the consequences cascade:

* Academic programs configured in ways that don’t reflect actual degree structures, creating degree audit failures
* Term models that don’t accommodate the institution’s full range of academic calendar variations—including non-standard terms, accelerated programs, and module-based formats
* Credit-hour definitions that conflict with financial aid enrollment intensity calculations
* Transfer credit articulation frameworks that don’t match the institution’s actual articulation policies

Each of these problems is difficult and expensive to remediate after go-live. Each of them is preventable if the registrar is shaping configuration decisions from the beginning.

---

## Academic Records Architecture Is a Long-Term Institutional Asset

The academic record is among the most consequential data an institution holds. It is the permanent documentation of a student’s academic career—the record that supports credential verification, transcript requests, graduate school applications, and professional licensing throughout a graduate’s lifetime.

ERP migration decisions about how academic records are structured, stored, and accessed are not technical decisions. They are institutional decisions with long-term consequences that only registrars have the professional standing to make.

Common academic records architecture risks in ERP migration include:

* Record conversion decisions that lose historical grading scale context
* Term and credit-hour structures that create anomalies in historical transcript presentation
* Document imaging and attachment frameworks that don’t meet transcript integrity standards
* Degree audit configuration that produces inaccurate graduation eligibility determinations

---

## Transfer Credit and Prior Learning Assessment Require Registrar Policy Authority

Transfer credit evaluation and prior learning assessment are areas where ERP configuration decisions are inseparable from institutional policy decisions.

The rules that govern transfer credit articulation—which courses transfer, how they apply to degree requirements, what limits apply—are registrar-owned policy decisions. The ERP system must be configured to reflect those policies accurately. When it is not—when configuration decisions are made by implementation staff without registrar policy guidance—the system enforces rules that the institution didn’t intend and can’t explain.

For institutions expanding prior learning assessment programs to serve adult learners, this is particularly high-stakes. PLA frameworks that don’t configure correctly in the ERP create operational problems that affect exactly the student population institutions most need to serve.

---

## A Leadership Question Worth Asking Now

The registrar’s office is not typically the loudest voice in ERP migration planning. Registrars are often understaffed, focused on current operational demands, and brought into technology projects after the architecture decisions they need to influence have already been made.

This pattern is not inevitable. It is a governance failure. And it produces ERP implementations that require expensive post-go-live remediation of configuration problems that a well-positioned registrar could have prevented.

**Are registrars shaping your ERP migration—or reacting to it after key decisions are made?**

The institutions that get this right bring the registrar to the table early, provide them with the authority to make binding configuration decisions in their domain, and treat their institutional knowledge as an irreplaceable asset in the implementation process. The institutions that don’t pay for it later.
