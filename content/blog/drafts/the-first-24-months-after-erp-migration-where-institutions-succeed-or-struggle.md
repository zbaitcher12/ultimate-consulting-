The first 24 months after ERP migration are the most consequential period in determining whether the investment delivers institutional value.

This is not widely understood. Most ERP migration planning focuses intensively on the period leading up to go-live—and relatively little on what comes after. The result is that institutions arrive at go-live with detailed plans for implementation and vague intentions for everything that follows.

Vague intentions don’t drive institutional transformation. Specific plans, dedicated investment, and accountability structures do. And the first 24 months are when those elements determine whether ERP migration produces the outcomes it promised.

---

## Months 1–6: Stabilization or Entrenchment

The first six months after go-live set the trajectory for everything that follows.

Institutions that invest in the stabilization period—maintaining elevated support capacity, resolving issues systematically, reinforcing training with real operational experience, and conducting regular cross-functional reviews—emerge from the first six months with a system that is performing reliably and a staff that is building genuine proficiency.

Institutions that scale back implementation resources immediately at go-live enter an entrenchment dynamic:

* Staff encountering operational problems with no support infrastructure develop workarounds that persist long after the problems could have been resolved
* Issues that required cross-functional coordination to resolve become departmental accommodations that don’t address root causes
* Leadership attention shifts away from the ERP to other institutional priorities before stabilization is complete
* The system becomes “good enough” rather than optimized, and the institutional expectation resets to a lower standard of performance

Recovering from entrenchment is possible but expensive. Preventing it requires investment in the first six months that is explicitly planned and resourced.

---

## Months 7–12: Adoption or Attrition

The second six months are when adoption patterns solidify.

Adoption in an ERP context means staff using the system as designed—routing workflows through the platform, using native reporting rather than manual extracts, handling exceptions through the system rather than around it. By month 12, adoption patterns are largely established. Workarounds that exist at month 12 tend to persist. Shadow systems that have re-emerged by month 12 tend to remain.

The institutions that achieve high adoption in months 7–12 do so through:

* Explicit adoption measurement, tracking not just login activity but actual workflow utilization
* Leadership accountability for system use in each functional area
* Continuing investment in role-specific training that addresses real operational scenarios
* Active identification and elimination of workarounds before they become embedded

---

## Months 13–24: Optimization or Stagnation

By the second year, institutions with strong stabilization and adoption are positioned to move into optimization—the phase where workflow redesign, automation, and analytics investment deliver the operational improvements that justified the migration.

Institutions without strong stabilization and adoption are in a different position. They are managing a system that works but doesn’t inspire confidence, carrying workarounds that undermine the system’s value, and facing a leadership team that is questioning whether the investment was worth it.

The difference between these two positions at month 24 is almost always traceable to decisions made in months 1–6. The institutions that invest in stabilization create the conditions for adoption. Adoption creates the conditions for optimization. And optimization is where ERP investment produces returns.

---

## Governance Maturity: The Infrastructure That Makes It All Work

Underlying stabilization, adoption, and optimization in the first 24 months is governance maturity—the development of the institutional decision-making infrastructure that makes the ERP system a managed asset rather than a managed liability.

Governance maturity in the first 24 months includes:

* Clear authority for configuration change decisions, with a review process that prevents unauthorized modifications
* Data governance frameworks that ensure ERP data remains trustworthy as the system evolves
* A technology roadmap that plans the next generation of ERP capability investments before current implementation value has degraded
* Vendor relationship management that treats the ERP vendor as a strategic partner rather than a support contract

---

## A Leadership Question Worth Asking Now

**Do you have a post-migration roadmap—or just a go-live plan?**

The first 24 months after ERP migration are not a denouement. They are a second project—one that requires its own planning, its own investment, and its own accountability structures.

The institutions that treat it that way consistently extract more value from their ERP migration than those that don’t. And the difference between those two outcomes is determined, in large part, in the first six months after go-live.
